01 Background

Sikorsky Aircraft was founded in 1923 and is the oldest helicopter company in the world. In fact the founder, Igor Sikorsky, produced the first viable helicopter in the United States in the 1930s. After a long and respected legacy of building universally-loved Black Hawks and presidential aircraft, Sikorsky ventured into autonomous aircraft in 2011. The team was able to acquire an S-76 commercial helicopter and outfitted it with fly-by-wire and advanced flight controls. A robust safety-critical software fallback system gained them FAA approval allow experimental software on the aircraft.

Gains in Sikorsky's autonomous aviation led DARPA to create the DARPA ALIAS (Aircrew Labor In-cockpit Automation System) program with Sikorsky, Aurora Flight Sciences, and Lockheed Martin. (At the time, Sikorsky belonged to United Technologies.) After three rounds, Sikorsky emerged as the sole competitor in the program.

One of the main reasons Sikorsky's system was so successful in the ALIAS program is because it was intended from the get-go to for commercialization, not for prototyping a one-off research platform. The core software was written to obey hard real-time constraints for the purpose of running in an RTOS, and the supporting software modules were written with safety-criticality and eventual certification in mind.

02 Design

I built this musical robot for David Nuñez — it will be used for researching the efficacy of music for forming relationships with robots. I put a lot of work into designing the robot's form around its function, an important component of human-robot interactions, as I learned. Many of the contours are inspired by those of old phonographs.

I began by sketching a few forms out on paper with David, then went through a few iterations in SolidWorks until the design was mechanically plausible and had an aesthetically pleasing yet functional form.

{% include image.html path="img/musicbot-concept.jpg" caption="Initial designs in SolidWorks." title="A CAD drawing of the musical robot in its early stages." %}

The parts were laser-cut and waterjet from stock sheets of acrylic and oak wood. The phonograph-like horn was 3D-printed and spray-painted with multiple coats.

After constructing the first prototype, I made many modifications to the design before building a second prototype with smoother joints and better motion overall.

03 Development

The final design features six degrees of freedom, which will allow the robot to move fluidly, a must in social robotics. Two of the motors are high-torque in order to manage the weight of the head of the robot; two more servos in the head are very small and lightweight in order to reduce the weight; and the last two servos are medium-sized to handle proper rotation of the body and the rolling of the head. This second design is more robust and puts less strain on the joints. David Nuñez will be taking the robot to the Opera of the Future group at the MIT Media Lab to perform experiments with.
